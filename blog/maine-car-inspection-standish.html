What Maine Inspectors Actually Check

Maine's inspection program is one of the more thorough in New England. Here's what we go through on every vehicle:

  • Brakes — pads, rotors, drums, lines, emergency brake function
  • Tires — tread depth, condition, no sidewall damage or bulges
  • Lights — headlights, taillights, brake lights, turn signals, reverse lights
  • Horn — must be functional and audible
  • Windshield wipers — must clear the windshield properly
  • Windshield glass — no cracks in driver's line of sight
  • Steering & suspension — no excessive play, worn ball joints, tie rods
  • Exhaust system — no leaks, catalytic converter present, no excessive smoke
  • OBD/emissions check — check engine light must be off, readiness monitors must be set
  • Seat belts — all belts must retract and latch properly
  • Mirrors — at least two functioning mirrors required
  • Body/frame condition — no sharp edges, holes in floor, severe rust on structural components

What Happens If You Fail?

In Maine, a failed inspection gives you 60 days to make the repairs and return for a free re-inspection. We'll give you a written list of exactly what failed and a no-pressure quote to fix it.

If you want to get the repairs done elsewhere, that's fine — we won't hold your paperwork hostage. You can come back for the re-inspection after.

The OBD / Emissions Check in Maine

If your check engine light is on, you will fail inspection — period. Maine uses the OBD II system to check your vehicle's readiness monitors. All monitors need to be "ready" and no fault codes can be active.

Common trap: People get their codes cleared at AutoZone, then come in for inspection — and still fail because the monitors haven't reset. We'll tell you upfront if this is likely before you waste your money on the inspection fee.
